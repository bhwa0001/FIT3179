Understanding land and marine ecological impacts

Our planet's landscapes are constantly changing. From forests to farmlands, grasslands to urban spaces. Understanding these shifts helps us comprehend humanity's footprint on Earth's ecosystems.

üåç How are our forests changing?

In the past 25 years, there has been drastic shifts in how our planet looks like.

In most places, there has been a slight decrease in total forest cover hinting at raised deforestation trends.

A few outliers should be noted, China has over 60,000,000 hectares MORE forest cover compared to 1990.

On the other side of the spectrum, Brazil has lost over 90,000,000 hectares of forest, mainly using the Amazon rainforest to fuel agricultural demands.

üå≥ What types of land cover in Australia?

Now let's explore the makeup of Australia's landcover throughout the past couple decades.

It seems for most of the 2000's, tree covered areas and herbaceous crops dominated the landscape. Interestingly grassland has seen a rise in land cover proportion in the past decade.

Touching back on the wildfires of 2019-2020, tree covered areas peaked in share of land cover. During this time, grasslands went from rank 1, all the way down to rank 4, back to rank 1 after the fires. We can conclude that while wildfires did contribute to significant deforestation, they affect grasslands even more.

Sidenote, 'other' land cover can refer to anything beyond the specified classes, such as artificial/man-made surfaces.

Australia has some of the largest and most diverse seagrass meadows in the world (Shark Bay, Great Barrier Reef, Spencer Gulf, etc.).

Queensland and Northern Territory is home to a majority of Australia's seagrass. However, it is estimated that over 30% has been lost since the 19th century. Preservation efforts are important in maintaining our healthy marine ecosystems.

Which countries are taking steps to improve?

Protected areas are an effective tool for biodiversity conservation and environmental management. These designated regions, including parks to marine reserves help to safeguard ecosystems. Over the past decade, the global commitment to expanding protected areas has increased, reflecting action taken against habitat loss and climate change.

We see that Europe has the highest proportion of protected areas against total land area. This might be due to European countries' relatively small sizes making it easier to achieve high %. Also, it is a very developed region which might contribute to more recognition and efforts to deal with ecological balance.

Less developed areas have lower % of protected areas. See Arab world, Caribbean/Pacific islands, South Asia. This could be due to countries in these regions prioritising socioeconomic development first.

Overall, there is an uplifting trend in protected areas and is a good step forward.
